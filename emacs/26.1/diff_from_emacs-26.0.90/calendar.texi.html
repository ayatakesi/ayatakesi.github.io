0005: @node Calendar/Diary
0006: @chapter The Calendar and the Diary
0007: @cindex calendar
0008: @findex calendar
0009: 
0010:   Emacs provides the functions of a desk calendar, with a diary of
0011: planned or past events.  It also has facilities for managing your
0012: appointments, and keeping track of how much time you spend working on
0013: certain projects.
0014: 
0015:   To enter the calendar, type @kbd{M-x calendar}; this displays a
0016: three-month calendar centered on the current month, with point on the
0017: current date.  With a numeric argument, as in @kbd{C-u M-x calendar}, it
0018: prompts you for the month and year to be the center of the three-month
0019: calendar.  The calendar uses its own buffer, whose major mode is
0020: Calendar mode.
0021: 
0022:   @kbd{mouse-3} in the calendar brings up a menu of operations on a
0023: particular date; @kbd{mouse-2} brings up a menu of commonly used
0024: calendar features that are independent of any particular date.  To exit
0025: the calendar, type @kbd{q}.
0026: 
0027: @iftex
0028:   This chapter describes the basic calendar features.
0029: For more advanced topics,
0030: @pxref{Advanced Calendar/Diary Usage,,, emacs-xtra, Specialized Emacs Features}.

0053: @node Calendar Motion
0054: @section Movement in the Calendar
0055: 
0056: @cindex moving inside the calendar
0057:   Calendar mode provides commands to move through the calendar in
0058: logical units of time such as days, weeks, months, and years.  If you
0059: move outside the three months originally displayed, the calendar
0060: display scrolls automatically through time to make the selected
0061: date visible.  Moving to a date lets you view its holidays or diary
0062: entries, or convert it to other calendars; moving by long time periods
0063: is also useful simply to scroll the calendar.
0064: 
0065: @menu
0066: * Calendar Unit Motion::      Moving by days, weeks, months, and years.
0067: * Move to Beginning or End::  Moving to start/end of weeks, months, and years.
0068: * Specified Dates::           Moving to the current date or another
0069:                                 specific date.
0070: @end menu

0072: @node Calendar Unit Motion
0073: @subsection Motion by Standard Lengths of Time
0074: 
0075:   The commands for movement in the calendar buffer parallel the
0076: commands for movement in text.  You can move forward and backward by
0077: days, weeks, months, and years.
0078: 
0079: @table @kbd
0080: @item C-f
0081: Move point one day forward (@code{calendar-forward-day}).
0082: @item C-b
0083: Move point one day backward (@code{calendar-backward-day}).
0084: @item C-n
0085: Move point one week forward (@code{calendar-forward-week}).
0086: @item C-p
0087: Move point one week backward (@code{calendar-backward-week}).
0088: @item M-@}
0089: Move point one month forward (@code{calendar-forward-month}).
0090: @item M-@{
0091: Move point one month backward (@code{calendar-backward-month}).
0092: @item C-x ]
0093: Move point one year forward (@code{calendar-forward-year}).
0094: @item C-x [
0095: Move point one year backward (@code{calendar-backward-year}).
0096: @end table
0097: 
0098: @kindex C-f @r{(Calendar mode)}
0099: @findex calendar-forward-day
0100: @kindex C-b @r{(Calendar mode)}
0101: @findex calendar-backward-day
0102: @kindex C-n @r{(Calendar mode)}
0103: @findex calendar-forward-week
0104: @kindex C-p @r{(Calendar mode)}
0105: @findex calendar-backward-week
0106:   The day and week commands are natural analogues of the usual Emacs
0107: commands for moving by characters and by lines.  Just as @kbd{C-n}
0108: usually moves to the same column in the following line, in Calendar
0109: mode it moves to the same day in the following week.  And @kbd{C-p}
0110: moves to the same day in the previous week.
0111: 
0112:   The arrow keys are equivalent to @kbd{C-f}, @kbd{C-b}, @kbd{C-n} and
0113: @kbd{C-p}, just as they normally are in other modes.
0114: 
0115: @kindex M-@} @r{(Calendar mode)}
0116: @findex calendar-forward-month
0117: @kindex M-@{ @r{(Calendar mode)}
0118: @findex calendar-backward-month
0119: @kindex C-x ] @r{(Calendar mode)}
0120: @findex calendar-forward-year
0121: @kindex C-x [ @r{(Calendar mode)}
0122: @findex calendar-forward-year
0123:   The commands for motion by months and years work like those for
0124: weeks, but move a larger distance.  The month commands @kbd{M-@}} and
0125: @kbd{M-@{} move forward or backward by an entire month.  The year
0126: commands @kbd{C-x ]} and @w{@kbd{C-x [}} move forward or backward a
0127: whole year.
0128: 
0129:   The easiest way to remember these commands is to consider months and
0130: years analogous to paragraphs and pages of text, respectively.  But
0131: the commands themselves are not quite analogous.  The ordinary Emacs
0132: paragraph commands move to the beginning or end of a paragraph,
0133: whereas these month and year commands move by an entire month or an
0134: entire year, keeping the same date within the month or year.
0135: 
0136:   All these commands accept a numeric argument as a repeat count.
0137: For convenience, the digit keys and the minus sign specify numeric
0138: arguments in Calendar mode even without the Meta modifier.  For example,
0139: @kbd{100 C-f} moves point 100 days forward from its present location.
0140:

0234: @node Scroll Calendar
0235: @section Scrolling in the Calendar
0236: 
0237: @cindex scrolling in the calendar
0238:   The calendar display scrolls automatically through time when you
0239: move out of the visible portion.  You can also scroll it manually.
0240: Imagine that the calendar window contains a long strip of paper with
0241: the months on it.  Scrolling the calendar means moving the strip
0242: horizontally, so that new months become visible in the window.
0243: 
0244: @table @kbd
0245: @item >
0246: Scroll calendar one month forward (@code{calendar-scroll-left}).
0247: @item <
0248: Scroll calendar one month backward (@code{calendar-scroll-right}).
0249: @item C-v
0250: @itemx @key{next}
0251: Scroll forward by three months (@code{calendar-scroll-left-three-months}).
0252: @item M-v
0253: @itemx @key{prior}
0254: Scroll backward by three months (@code{calendar-scroll-right-three-months}).
0255: @end table
0256: 
0257: @kindex > @r{(Calendar mode)}
0258: @findex calendar-scroll-left
0259: @kindex < @r{(Calendar mode)}
0260: @findex calendar-scroll-right
0261:   The most basic calendar scroll commands scroll by one month at a
0262: time.  This means that there are two months of overlap between the
0263: display before the command and the display after.  @kbd{>} scrolls the
0264: calendar contents one month forward in time.  @kbd{<} scrolls the
0265: contents one month backwards in time.
0266: 
0267: @kindex C-v @r{(Calendar mode)}
0268: @findex calendar-scroll-left-three-months
0269: @kindex M-v @r{(Calendar mode)}
0270: @findex calendar-scroll-right-three-months
0271:   The commands @kbd{C-v} and @kbd{M-v} scroll the calendar by an entire
0272: screenful---three months---in analogy with the usual meaning of
0273: these commands.  @kbd{C-v} makes later dates visible and @kbd{M-v} makes
0274: earlier dates visible.  These commands take a numeric argument as a
0275: repeat count; in particular, since @kbd{C-u} multiplies the next command
0276: by four, typing @kbd{C-u C-v} scrolls the calendar forward by a year and
0277: typing @kbd{C-u M-v} scrolls the calendar backward by a year.
0278: 
0279:   The function keys @key{next} and @key{prior} are equivalent to
0280: @kbd{C-v} and @kbd{M-v}, just as they are in other modes.

0349: @node Writing Calendar Files
0350: @section Writing Calendar Files
0351: 
0352:   You can write calendars and diary entries to HTML and @LaTeX{} files.
0353: 
0354: @cindex calendar and HTML
0355:   The Calendar HTML commands produce files of HTML code that contain
0356: calendar, holiday, and diary entries.  Each file applies to one month,
0357: and has a name of the format @file{@var{yyyy}-@var{mm}.html}, where
0358: @var{yyyy} and @var{mm} are the four-digit year and two-digit month,
0359: respectively.  The variable @code{cal-html-directory} specifies the
0360: default output directory for the HTML files.  To prevent holidays
0361: from being shown, customize @code{cal-html-holidays}.
0362: 
0363: @vindex cal-html-css-default
0364:   Diary entries enclosed by @code{<} and @code{>} are interpreted as
0365: HTML tags (for example: this is a diary entry with <font
0366: color=''red''>some red text</font>).  You can change the overall
0367: appearance of the displayed HTML pages (for example, the color of
0368: various page elements, header styles) via a stylesheet @file{cal.css} in
0369: the directory containing the HTML files (see the value of the variable
0370: @code{cal-html-css-default} for relevant style settings).
0371: 
0372: @kindex t @r{(Calendar mode)}
0373: @table @kbd
0374: @item H m
0375: Generate a one-month calendar (@code{cal-html-cursor-month}).
0376: @item H y
0377: Generate a calendar file for each month of a year, as well as an index
0378: page (@code{cal-html-cursor-year}).  By default, this command writes
0379: files to a @var{yyyy} subdirectory---if this is altered some hyperlinks
0380: between years will not work.
0381: @end table
0382: 
0383:   If the variable @code{cal-html-print-day-number-flag} is
0384: non-@code{nil}, then the monthly calendars show the day-of-the-year
0385: number.  The variable @code{cal-html-year-index-cols} specifies the
0386: number of columns in the yearly index page.
0387:

0389:   The Calendar @LaTeX{} commands produce a buffer of @LaTeX{} code that
0390: prints as a calendar.  Depending on the command you use, the printed
0391: calendar covers the day, week, month or year that point is in.
0392:

0394: @table @kbd

0400: @item t d

0402: (@code{cal-tex-cursor-day}).

0689: @node Calendar Systems
0690: @subsection Supported Calendar Systems
0691: 
0692: @cindex ISO commercial calendar
0693:   The ISO commercial calendar is often used in business.
0694: 
0695: @cindex Julian calendar
0696:   The Julian calendar, named after Julius Caesar, was the one used in Europe
0697: throughout medieval times, and in many countries up until the nineteenth
0698: century.
0699: 
0700: @cindex Julian day numbers
0701: @cindex astronomical day numbers
0702:   Astronomers use a simple counting of days elapsed since noon, Monday,
0703: January 1, 4713 B.C. on the Julian calendar.  The number of days elapsed
0704: is called the @dfn{Julian day number} or the @dfn{Astronomical day number}.
0705: 
0706: @cindex Hebrew calendar
0707:   The Hebrew calendar is used by tradition in the Jewish religion.  The
0708: Emacs calendar program uses the Hebrew calendar to determine the dates
0709: of Jewish holidays.  Hebrew calendar dates begin and end at sunset.
0710: 
0711: @cindex Islamic calendar
0712:   The Islamic calendar is used in many predominantly Islamic countries.
0713: Emacs uses it to determine the dates of Islamic holidays.  There is no
0714: universal agreement in the Islamic world about the calendar; Emacs uses
0715: a widely accepted version, but the precise dates of Islamic holidays
0716: often depend on proclamation by religious authorities, not on
0717: calculations.  As a consequence, the actual dates of observance can vary
0718: slightly from the dates computed by Emacs.  Islamic calendar dates begin
0719: and end at sunset.
0720: 
0721: @cindex French Revolutionary calendar
0722:   The French Revolutionary calendar was created by the Jacobins after the 1789
0723: revolution, to represent a more secular and nature-based view of the annual
0724: cycle, and to install a 10-day week in a rationalization measure similar to
0725: the metric system.  The French government officially abandoned this
0726: calendar at the end of 1805.
0727: 
0728: @cindex Mayan calendar
0729:   The Maya of Central America used three separate, overlapping calendar
0730: systems, the @emph{long count}, the @emph{tzolkin}, and the @emph{haab}.
0731: Emacs knows about all three of these calendars.  Experts dispute the
0732: exact correlation between the Mayan calendar and our calendar; Emacs uses the
0733: Goodman-Martinez-Thompson correlation in its calculations.
0734: 
0735: @cindex Coptic calendar
0736: @cindex Ethiopic calendar
0737:   The Copts use a calendar based on the ancient Egyptian solar calendar.
0738: Their calendar consists of twelve 30-day months followed by an extra
0739: five-day period.  Once every fourth year they add a leap day to this
0740: extra period to make it six days.  The Ethiopic calendar is identical in
0741: structure, but has different year numbers and month names.
0742: 
0743: @cindex Persian calendar
0744:   The Persians use a solar calendar based on a design of Omar Khayyam.
0745: Their calendar consists of twelve months of which the first six have 31
0746: days, the next five have 30 days, and the last has 29 in ordinary years
0747: and 30 in leap years.  Leap years occur in a complicated pattern every
0748: four or five years.
0749: The calendar implemented here is the arithmetical Persian calendar
0750: championed by Birashk, based on a 2,820-year cycle.  It differs from
0751: the astronomical Persian calendar, which is based on astronomical
0752: events.  As of this writing the first future discrepancy is projected
0753: to occur on March 20, 2025.  It is currently not clear what the
0754: official calendar of Iran will be at that time.
0755: @c FIXME not so far in the future now.
0756: 
0757: @cindex Chinese calendar
0758:   The Chinese calendar is a complicated system of lunar months arranged
0759: into solar years.  The years go in cycles of sixty, each year containing
0760: either twelve months in an ordinary year or thirteen months in a leap
0761: year; each month has either 29 or 30 days.  Years, ordinary months, and
0762: days are named by combining one of ten @dfn{celestial stems} with one of
0763: twelve @dfn{terrestrial branches} for a total of sixty names that are
0764: repeated in a cycle of sixty.
0765: 
0766: @cindex Bahá'í calendar
0767:   The Bahá'í calendar system is based on a solar cycle of 19 months with
0768: 19 days each.  The four remaining intercalary days are placed
0769: between the 18th and 19th months.

1125: @node Date Formats
1126: @subsection Date Formats
1127: 
1128:   Here are some sample diary entries, illustrating different ways of
1129: formatting a date.  The examples all show dates in American order
1130: (month, day, year), but Calendar mode supports European order (day,
1131: month, year) and ISO order (year, month, day) as options.
1132: 
1133: @example
1134: 4/20/12  Switch-over to new tabulation system
1135: apr. 25  Start tabulating annual results
1136: 4/30  Results for April are due
1137: */25  Monthly cycle finishes
1138: Friday  Don't leave without backing up files
1139: @end example
1140: 
1141:   The first entry appears only once, on April 20, 2012.  The second and
1142: third appear every year on the specified dates, and the fourth uses a
1143: wildcard (asterisk) for the month, so it appears on the 25th of every
1144: month.  The final entry appears every week on Friday.
1145: 
1146:   You can use just numbers to express a date, as in
1147: @samp{@var{month}/@var{day}} or @samp{@var{month}/@var{day}/@var{year}}.
1148: This must be followed by a nondigit.  In the date itself, @var{month}
1149: and @var{day} are numbers of one or two digits.  The optional @var{year}
1150: is also a number, and may be abbreviated to the last two digits; that
1151: is, you can use @samp{11/12/2012} or @samp{11/12/12}.
1152: 
1153:   Dates can also have the form @samp{@var{monthname} @var{day}} or
1154: @samp{@var{monthname} @var{day}, @var{year}}, where the month's name can
1155: be spelled in full or abbreviated (with or without a period).  The
1156: preferred abbreviations for month and day names can be set using
1157: the variables @code{calendar-abbrev-length},
1158: @code{calendar-month-abbrev-array}, and
1159: @code{calendar-day-abbrev-array}.  The default is to use the first three
1160: letters of a name as its abbreviation.  Case is not significant.
1161: 
1162:   A date may be @dfn{generic}; that is, partially unspecified.  Then the
1163: entry applies to all dates that match the specification.  If the date
1164: does not contain a year, it is generic and applies to any year.
1165: Alternatively, @var{month}, @var{day}, or @var{year} can be @samp{*};
1166: this matches any month, day, or year, respectively.  Thus, a diary entry
1167: @samp{3/*/*} matches any day in March of any year; so does @samp{march
1168: *}.
1169: 
1170: @vindex calendar-date-style
1171: @findex calendar-set-date-style
1172:   If you prefer the European style of writing dates (in which the day
1173: comes before the month), or the ISO style (in which the order is year,
1174: month, day), type @kbd{M-x calendar-set-date-style} while in the
1175: calendar, or customize the variable @code{calendar-date-style}.  This
1176: affects how diary dates are interpreted, date display, and the order in
1177: which some commands expect their arguments to be given.
1178: 
1179:   You can use the name of a day of the week as a generic date which
1180: applies to any date falling on that day of the week.  You can abbreviate
1181: the day of the week as described above, or spell it in full; case is not
1182: significant.

1240: @node Special Diary Entries
1241: @subsection Special Diary Entries
1242: 
1243:   In addition to entries based on calendar dates, the diary file can
1244: contain @dfn{sexp entries} for regular events such as anniversaries.
1245: These entries are based on Lisp expressions (sexps) that Emacs evaluates
1246: as it scans the diary file.  Instead of a date, a sexp entry contains
1247: @samp{%%} followed by a Lisp expression which must begin and end with
1248: parentheses.  The Lisp expression determines which dates the entry
1249: applies to.
1250: 
1251:   Calendar mode provides commands to insert certain commonly used
1252: sexp entries:
1253: 
1254: @table @kbd
1255: @item i a
1256: Add an anniversary diary entry for the selected date
1257: (@code{diary-insert-anniversary-entry}).
1258: @item i b
1259: Add a block diary entry for the current region
1260: (@code{diary-insert-block-entry}).
1261: @item i c
1262: Add a cyclic diary entry starting at the date
1263: (@code{diary-insert-cyclic-entry}).
1264: @end table
1265: 
1266: @kindex i a @r{(Calendar mode)}
1267: @findex diary-insert-anniversary-entry
1268:   If you want to make a diary entry that applies to the anniversary of a
1269: specific date, move point to that date and use the @kbd{i a} command.
1270: This displays the end of your diary file in another window and inserts
1271: the anniversary description; you can then type the rest of the diary
1272: entry.  The entry looks like this:
1273: 
1274: @findex diary-anniversary
1275: @example
1276: %%(diary-anniversary 10 31 1988) Arthur's birthday
1277: @end example
1278: 
1279: @noindent
1280: This entry applies to October 31 in any year after 1988; @samp{10 31
1281: 1988} specifies the date.  (If you are using the European or ISO
1282: calendar style, the input order of month, day and year is different.)
1283: The reason this expression requires a beginning year is that advanced
1284: diary functions can use it to calculate the number of elapsed years.
1285: 
1286:   A @dfn{block} diary entry applies to a specified range of consecutive
1287: dates.  Here is a block diary entry that applies to all dates from June
1288: 24, 2012 through July 10, 2012:
1289: 
1290: @findex diary-block
1291: @example
1292: %%(diary-block 6 24 2012 7 10 2012) Vacation
1293: @end example
1294: 
1295: @noindent
1296: The @samp{6 24 2012} indicates the starting date and the @samp{7 10 2012}
1297: indicates the stopping date.  (Again, if you are using the European or ISO
1298: calendar style, the input order of month, day and year is different.)
1299: 
1300: @kindex i b @r{(Calendar mode)}
1301: @findex diary-insert-block-entry
1302:   To insert a block entry, place point and the mark on the two
1303: dates that begin and end the range, and type @kbd{i b}.  This command
1304: displays the end of your diary file in another window and inserts the
1305: block description; you can then type the diary entry.
1306: 
1307: @kindex i c @r{(Calendar mode)}
1308: @findex diary-insert-cyclic-entry
1309:    @dfn{Cyclic} diary entries repeat after a fixed interval of days.  To
1310: create one, select the starting date and use the @kbd{i c} command.  The
1311: command prompts for the length of interval, then inserts the entry,
1312: which looks like this:
1313: 
1314: @findex diary-cyclic
1315: @example
1316: %%(diary-cyclic 50 3 1 2012) Renew medication
1317: @end example
1318: 
1319: @noindent
1320: This entry applies to March 1, 2012 and every 50th day following;
1321: @samp{3 1 2012} specifies the starting date.  (If you are using the
1322: European or ISO calendar style, the input order of month, day and year
1323: is different.)
1324: 
1325:   All three of these commands make marking diary entries.  To insert a
1326: nonmarking entry, give a prefix argument to the command.  For example,
1327: @kbd{C-u i a} makes a nonmarking anniversary diary entry.
1328: 
1329:   Marking sexp diary entries in the calendar can be time-consuming,
1330: since every date visible in the calendar window must be individually
1331: checked.  So it's a good idea to make sexp diary entries nonmarking
1332: (with @samp{&}) when possible.
1333: 
1334:   Another sophisticated kind of sexp entry, a @dfn{floating} diary entry,
1335: specifies a regularly occurring event by offsets specified in days,
1336: weeks, and months.  It is comparable to a crontab entry interpreted by
1337: the @code{cron} utility.  Here is a nonmarking, floating diary entry
1338: that applies to the fourth Thursday in November:
1339: 
1340: @findex diary-float
1341: @example
1342: &%%(diary-float 11 4 4) American Thanksgiving
1343: @end example
1344: 
1345: @noindent
1346: The 11 specifies November (the eleventh month), the 4 specifies Thursday
1347: (the fourth day of the week, where Sunday is numbered zero), and the
1348: second 4 specifies the fourth Thursday (1 would mean ``first'', 2 would
1349: mean ``second'', @minus{}2 would mean ``second-to-last'', and so on).
1350: The month can be a single month or a list of months.  Thus you could change
1351: the 11 above to @samp{'(1 2 3)} and have the entry apply to the last
1352: Thursday of January, February, and March.  If the month is @code{t}, the
1353: entry applies to all months of the year.
1354: 
1355:   Each of the standard sexp diary entries takes an optional parameter
1356: specifying the name of a face or a single-character string to use when
1357: marking the entry in the calendar.  Most generally, sexp diary entries
1358: can perform arbitrary computations to determine when they apply.

1512: @node Daylight Saving
1513: @section Daylight Saving Time
1514: @cindex daylight saving time
1515: 
1516:   Emacs understands the difference between standard time and daylight
1517: saving time---the times given for sunrise, sunset, solstices,
1518: equinoxes, and the phases of the moon take that into account.  The rules
1519: for daylight saving time vary from place to place and have also varied
1520: historically from year to year.  To do the job properly, Emacs needs to
1521: know which rules to use.
1522: 
1523: @vindex calendar-daylight-savings-starts
1524: @vindex calendar-daylight-savings-ends
1525:   Some operating systems keep track of the rules that apply to the place
1526: where you are; on these systems, Emacs gets the information it needs
1527: from the system automatically.  If some or all of this information is
1528: missing, Emacs fills in the gaps with the rules currently used in
1529: Cambridge, Massachusetts.  If the resulting rules are not what you want,
1530: you can tell Emacs the rules to use by setting certain variables:
1531: @code{calendar-daylight-savings-starts} and
1532: @code{calendar-daylight-savings-ends}.
1533: 
1534:   These values should be Lisp expressions that refer to the variable
1535: @code{year}, and evaluate to the Gregorian date on which daylight
1536: saving time starts or (respectively) ends, in the form of a list
1537: @code{(@var{month} @var{day} @var{year})}.  The values should be
1538: @code{nil} if your area does not use daylight saving time.
1539: 
1540:   Emacs uses these expressions to determine the starting date of
1541: daylight saving time for the holiday list and for correcting times of
1542: day in the solar and lunar calculations.
1543: 
1544:   The values for Cambridge, Massachusetts are as follows:
1545: 
1546: @example
1547: (calendar-nth-named-day 2 0 3 year)
1548: (calendar-nth-named-day 1 0 11 year)
1549: @end example
1550: 
1551: @noindent
1552: That is, the second 0th day (Sunday) of the third month (March) in
1553: the year specified by @code{year}, and the first Sunday of the eleventh month
1554: (November) of that year.  If daylight saving time were
1555: changed to start on October 1, you would set
1556: @code{calendar-daylight-savings-starts} to this:
1557: 
1558: @example
1559: (list 10 1 year)
1560: @end example
1561: 
1562:   If there is no daylight saving time at your location, or if you want
1563: all times in standard time, set @code{calendar-daylight-savings-starts}
1564: and @code{calendar-daylight-savings-ends} to @code{nil}.
1565: 
1566: @vindex calendar-daylight-time-offset
1567:   The variable @code{calendar-daylight-time-offset} specifies the
1568: difference between daylight saving time and standard time, measured in
1569: minutes.  The value for Cambridge, Massachusetts is 60.
1570: 
1571: @c @vindex calendar-daylight-savings-starts-time  too long!
1572: @vindex calendar-daylight-savings-ends-time
1573:   Finally, the two variables
1574: @code{calendar-daylight-savings-starts-time} and
1575: @code{calendar-daylight-savings-ends-time} specify the number of
1576: minutes after midnight local time when the transition to and from
1577: daylight saving time should occur.  For Cambridge, Massachusetts both
1578: variables' values are 120.
